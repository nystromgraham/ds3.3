Gender Equality, Quality Education, and the Global Decline in Fertility Rates

Chapter 5 The Confluence of SDGs #4 and #5 — Fertility Rates as a Signal of Progress

The SDG framework for measuring progress in equitable and sustainable development requires frequent progress assessments through data analysis. A notable demographic trend has accompanied progress on gender equality and education — global fertility rates are declining. Reports show a drop from roughly five children per woman in 1965 to about 2.5 today. Researchers attribute this shift primarily to three factors: women’s empowerment and access to education, declining child mortality, and the rising cost of raising children (Roser, 2014). The relationship between women’s education and lower fertility is well-established across many contexts. Jain (1981) identified this pattern across twelve countries, and Martín (1995) confirmed it across 26 demographic and health surveys, finding that higher education is consistently associated with lower fertility, later marriage, and greater contraceptive use — all indicators of women’s reproductive autonomy. Another study found the same inverse relationship in China, and proposed that declining fertility rates can themselves serve as a novel indicator of progress toward SDGs #4 and #5 (Chen & Guo, 2022).
At the individual level, a recent article from the New York Times found that women in the United States are delaying childbearing not out of indifference, but out of a desire for financial stability and readiness: “I want to be financially stable and in a place I can call my own… I saw my parents get stressed over money, and I don’t want my kid to experience that.” (Tavernise & Adelson, 2026). This reflects a broader shift in more developed nations; women exercising the informed, autonomous decision-making that the SDG framework was designed to enable. The global decline in fertility rates is less a cause for concern than a sign that decades of investment in gender equality and education are beginning to take hold, particularly in more developed nations across the globe.
